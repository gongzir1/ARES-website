Abstract

Federated Learning (FL) enables collaborative model training by sharing model updates instead of raw data, aiming to protect user privacy. However, recent studies reveal that these shared updates can inadvertently leak sensitive training data through gradient inversion attacks (GIAs). Among them, active GIAs are particularly powerful, enabling high-fidelity reconstruction of individual samples even under large batch sizes. Nevertheless, existing approaches often require architectural modifications, which limit their practical applicability. In this work, we bridge this gap by introducing the Activation REcovery via Sparse inversion (ARES) attack, an active GIA designed to reconstruct training samples from large training batches without requiring architectural modifications. Specifically, we formulate the recovery problem as a noisy sparse recovery task and solve it using the generalized Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (Lasso). To extend the attack to multi-sample recovery, ARES incorporates the imprint method to disentangle activations, enabling scalable per-sample reconstruction. We further establish the expected recovery rate and derive an upper bound on the reconstruction error, providing theoretical guarantees for the ARES attack. Extensive experiments on CNNs and MLPs demonstrate that ARES achieves high-fidelity reconstruction across diverse datasets, significantly outperforming prior GIAs under large batch sizes and realistic FL settings. Our results highlight that intermediate activations pose a serious and underestimated privacy risk in FL, underscoring the urgent need for stronger defenses.

Problem Statement

In Federated Learning (FL), clients collaboratively train a shared model by exchanging model updates (e.g., gradients) with a central server, keeping their raw data local. While FL is designed to preserve privacy, malicious attackers can still extract sensitive private data from these shared updates through Gradient Inversion Attacks (GIAs).

While powerful active GIAs can recover training samples even under large batch sizes, they typically rely on deploying non-standard, specially crafted network architectures to the client devices. This creates a critical research question: how can an attacker reconstruct private data from large batches in practical FL settings without altering the standard architecture? Our ARES attack exploits the intermediate activations of fully connected layers to bridge this gap, posing a highly realistic privacy threat.

Method Overview

The ARES attack operates in two main stages: Preparation and Inference.

During the preparation stage, the attacker configures the global model parameters before distribution. Specifically, it maliciously crafts the weights and biases of the fully connected (FC) layer to maximize information leakage, ensuring each neuron leaves a distinct imprint corresponding to a single client’s data sample.

During the inference stage, once the locally-trained updates are returned by the clients, the attacker utilizes linear layer leakage to extract the intermediate activations fed into the FC layer. To faithfully map these activations back to the original training images without requiring architecture modifications, the recovery is reformulated as a noisy sparse recovery problem. This is solved effectively using the generalized Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (Lasso) algorithm.
